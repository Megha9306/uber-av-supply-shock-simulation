NOTE 01

Dispatch Note

ATTACHED · JUN 29–30, 2026 Waymo and Uber ended their autonomous vehicle pilot partnership in Phoenix, pulling a slice of the market's driverless capacity off Uber's platform overnight. This report exists to reason about exactly that kind of event: what happens to a rideshare marketplace when supply drops suddenly, and does every hour of the day feel it the same?
NOTE 02

Meter Log

Six readings, taken in order, each one correcting or building on the last. Two of them are logged because an earlier assumption turned out wrong.

01

Isolate Uber trips

14.4M trips filtered from NYC's public High Volume For-Hire Vehicle records (April 2025), tagged by license code HV0003.

02

Build a wait-time baseline

Wait time logged as the gap between request and pickup, rolled up across roughly 6,200 zone-hour readings.

03

Regress wait on demand DEAD END

A demand-only model explained almost nothing (R² = 0.04). The relationship was U-shaped, not linear, driven by time of day rather than trip volume.

04

Segment by time of day

Rebuilding with time-of-day buckets and borough effects lifted R² to 0.34. Overnight and late-night hours came out significantly worse than any other window (p < .001).

05

Run the shock, get it backwards CAUGHT & FIXED

Feeding the regression's demand coefficient straight into the shock implied wait time would fall as demand rose. That's the observed, not caused, relationship. Supply and demand move together naturally in the data; a real shock holds supply fixed.

06

Simulate with supply held fixed

Reworked using a queueing-style elasticity: wait time scales non-linearly with the demand-to-supply ratio, shock severity weighted by how tight each hour's supply already ran.

NOTE 03

Readout

Simulating a 20% loss of vehicle supply across a 24-hour window, holding demand's natural shape constant.

A uniform 20% supply loss doesn't land uniformly. Hours already running thin on drivers, midnight to 4am, take roughly 50–55% longer waits, while midday and early evening, where supply ran deeper to start, absorb the same shock at closer to 35–40%.

NOTE 04

Fine Print

VOID WHERE NOTED · READ BEFORE CITING
  1. NYC stands in for Phoenix. Phoenix-level trip data isn't public. The method carries over directly if it were.
  2. No real vehicle-supply data. Supply is inferred from wait-time patterns, not observed, a modeled assumption rather than a driver count.
  3. Borough, not zone, fixed effects. A full 265-zone model was numerically unstable given sparse low-volume zones, so the analysis steps down to five boroughs.
  4. 20% shock size is illustrative. Chosen as a plausible scenario, not derived from Waymo's actual reported share of Phoenix capacity.
